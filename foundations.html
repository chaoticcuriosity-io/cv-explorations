Foundations: start here

Everything you need to understand the rest of this site — assuming no prior knowledge

If you’re new to computer vision (or to machine learning, or even to a lot of programming), read this page first. It builds up — from “what is an image to a computer” to “how do these models work and how do we measure them” — every idea the module reports lean on. Nothing here assumes you’ve seen it before. There’s a one-line glossary for quick lookups and a model families page that introduces each system by name (YOLO, DINO, SAM, …).

What is computer vision?

Computer vision (CV) is the field of getting computers to extract meaning from images and video — the way your eyes and brain do without effort. “Is there a person in this photo? Where? How far away? What are they doing?” are all trivial for you and were, until recently, nearly impossible for a computer. This site is a tour of the modern techniques that changed that.

How does a computer “see” an image?

A computer has no eyes. To it, an image is just a big grid of numbers.

  • A digital image is a grid of pixels (picture elements). A phone photo might be 4000 × 3000 pixels — 12 million of them.
  • Each pixel has a colour, stored as three numbers: how much Red, Green, and Blue it contains, each from 0 to 255. White is (255, 255, 255); pure red is (255, 0, 0). These three are called channels.
  • So a colour image is really a stack of three grids of numbers — height × width × 3. That block of numbers is called a tensor (just a fancy word for a multi-dimensional array of numbers).

That’s the entire input. Every model on this site takes in that grid of numbers and outputs other numbers — a label, box coordinates, a depth value per pixel. All of computer vision is turning one set of numbers into another, usefully.

What is a “model”?

A model is a mathematical function with a huge number of adjustable knobs. You feed numbers in (the image), it does a long chain of arithmetic, and numbers come out (the answer). The knobs — there can be millions or billions of them — are called parameters or weights.

The magic is how the knobs get set. Nobody sets them by hand. Instead:

  • A neural network is a particular kind of model, loosely inspired by brain neurons: it’s built from many simple units arranged in layers. Early layers detect simple things (edges, colours); later layers combine those into complex things (an eye, a wheel, a face). “Deep learning” just means a neural network with many layers.
  • The weights start out random — the model knows nothing. Then we train it.

Training vs. inference (the two phases)

This distinction is everywhere, so it’s worth nailing:

  • Training is the learning phase. We show the model thousands of examples for which we already know the right answer (e.g. images each labelled “cat” or “dog” — those known answers are called labels or ground truth). Each time the model guesses, we measure how wrong it was (the loss) and nudge every weight a tiny bit in the direction that would have made it less wrong. Repeat millions of times and the random knobs slowly become a working cat-vs-dog detector. The nudging algorithm is gradient descent; one full pass over the training data is an epoch.
  • Inference is the using phase. Training is done, the weights are frozen, and you just feed in a new image to get an answer. Inference is fast (milliseconds); training is slow (minutes to weeks).

A model whose weights have already been trained (often by a big lab, on millions of images) is pretrained. A saved set of trained weights is a checkpoint (you’ll see files like yolo26n.pt). Almost everything on this site uses pretrained models — you download someone’s finished weights and run inference. Module 10 is the exception: it trains (specifically, fine-tunes — see below).

Why GPUs?

All this arithmetic is the same simple operation (multiply-and-add) done billions of times in parallel. A GPU (graphics card) is hardware built to do exactly that, thousands of operations at once, so it runs these models 10–100× faster than a normal CPU. The model’s weights have to fit in the GPU’s memory (VRAM); that’s why you’ll see “24 GB” mentioned — it caps how big a model you can run. Everything here runs on a 24 GB NVIDIA RTX 3090 Ti.

The two great architectures

Almost every model here is built on one of two designs (or a mix). You don’t need the math, just the intuition.

CNNs (Convolutional Neural Networks)

A CNN is the classic image architecture (dominant from 2012). Its key trick is the convolution: it slides a small window (a filter) across the image looking for one pattern — say, a vertical edge — and reports where it found it. Stack many of these: the first layer finds edges, the next combines edges into textures and corners, the next into parts (an eye, a wheel), the next into whole objects. Because the same small filter is reused across the whole image, CNNs are efficient and naturally handle “the cat could be anywhere in the frame.” YOLO (modules 01, 03, 05, 06) is the famous CNN-family detector.

Transformers and “attention” (and ViTs)

The transformer (from 2017, originally for language — it’s the “T” in ChatGPT) now dominates vision too. Its key trick is attention: every part of the input can directly “look at” every other part and decide what’s relevant. For an image, a Vision Transformer (ViT) chops the picture into a grid of small patches, turns each into numbers, and lets all patches attend to one another — so the model can relate a hand on one side of the image to a tool on the other in a single step. CNNs build understanding up locally; transformers can use the whole image’s context at once. DETR / RF-DETR (module 01), DINO (module 07), SAM (module 03), and the vision-language models (module 08) are all transformer-based.

Backbone vs. head

A common pattern: a backbone (a CNN or ViT) chews the image into a rich numerical summary of “what’s in it” (called features or an embedding), and a small head on top turns those features into the specific answer for your task (a class, boxes, a depth map). Swap the head, reuse the backbone, and you can do a different task — which is why one good backbone (like DINO) shows up behind many models.

The core tasks (one per kind of question)

Each module answers a different question about an image. From simplest to richest:

Task The question Output Module
Classification What is this, overall? one label 02
Object detection What objects, and where? labelled boxes 01
Segmentation Which exact pixels? per-pixel masks 03
Depth How far is each pixel? a distance per pixel 04
Pose Where are the body joints? keypoints (a skeleton) 05
Tracking Same object across video frames? boxes with persistent IDs 06
Retrieval / embeddings How similar are two images? a comparable vector 07
Vision-language Describe / answer / reason free-form text 08

A bounding box (“box”) is just a rectangle around an object, given as four numbers (left, top, right, bottom). A mask is a yes/no for every pixel (“is this pixel part of the dog?”). A keypoint is a single marked point (e.g. “left elbow”).

How we measure success (metrics)

A core promise of this site is honest numbers, so you need to know what they mean.

For classification:

  • Accuracy — the fraction it got right. Top-1 accuracy = the model’s single best guess was correct. Top-5 accuracy = the right answer was among its top five guesses (useful when many classes look alike).

For detection (and anything with boxes):

  • IoU (Intersection over Union) — how well a predicted box overlaps the true box. Imagine the two rectangles; IoU = (area where they overlap) ÷ (area they cover together). 1.0 = perfect overlap, 0 = no overlap. A prediction usually “counts” if IoU ≥ 0.5.
  • Precision — of the boxes the model predicted, what fraction were correct? (Penalises false positives — crying wolf.)
  • Recall — of the objects that were really there, what fraction did the model find? (Penalises false negatives — misses.)
  • There’s always a trade-off: a model can catch everything by guessing wildly (high recall, low precision) or only report sure things (high precision, low recall). A confidence score (the model’s own 0–1 certainty per prediction) and a threshold control where you sit.
  • mAP (mean Average Precision) — the standard single detection score, from 0 to 1 (often shown as a %). It sweeps the confidence threshold to trace the full precision-vs-recall curve, takes the area under it (that’s Average Precision), and averages across all object classes. COCO mAP averages this over ten overlap strictnesses (IoU 0.5 to 0.95), so it rewards tight boxes, not just roughly-right ones. Higher = better; ~0.5 is strong for everyday photos.

For speed:

  • Latency — how long one image takes, in milliseconds (ms). Lower = faster.
  • FPS (frames per second) / throughput — how many images per second. ~30 FPS is real-time video. Latency and FPS are two views of the same thing (30 FPS ≈ 33 ms each).

Accuracy and speed usually trade off against each other — a recurring theme in the reports is that there’s no single “best” model, only different points on that curve.

Jargon you’ll meet everywhere

Short plain-language versions (the glossary has the rest):

  • NMS (Non-Maximum Suppression) — detectors often fire several overlapping boxes on one object. NMS is the clean-up step that keeps the most confident box and deletes its duplicates. “NMS-free” (e.g. YOLO26) means the model learned to emit one box per object directly, skipping that step — simpler and faster.
  • Anchor — older detectors guessed by adjusting a fixed set of pre-defined template boxes (“anchors”) tiled over the image. “Anchor-free” models drop that crutch and predict boxes directly.
  • Embedding — a list of numbers (a vector) that summarises an image (or patch, or word) so that similar things get similar vectors. Comparing images becomes comparing vectors. The heart of module 07.
  • Zero-shot — using a model on a task or set of categories it was never explicitly trained on, by description rather than by examples. CLIP can classify “a photo of a tractor” without ever having a “tractor” training label (module 02).
  • Open-vocabulary — a detector/segmenter you prompt with free text (“find the forklift”) instead of a fixed list of classes (module 01).
  • Foundation model — a large model pretrained on enormous, broad data, meant to be reused for many tasks (often by prompting), rather than trained for one. SAM, CLIP, and the VLMs are examples.
  • Fine-tuning — taking a pretrained model and training it a bit more on your specific data to specialise it (cheaper than training from scratch). Module 10.
  • Augmentation — making more training examples by randomly flipping/cropping/recolouring the ones you have, so the model generalises better.
  • Inference vs training, weights/checkpoint, epoch, VRAM — defined above.

The big arc of the field (why the models are the way they are)

This whole site is, in a sense, a history in fast-forward:

  1. Classical CV (pre-2012). Humans hand-designed the rules — explicit math to find edges, corners, and motion. Fast and transparent, but blind to meaning (“edge” yes, “cat” no). This is module 09, shown last as the illuminating contrast.
  2. The deep-learning revolution (2012). A CNN crushed the ImageNet image-classification contest, and the field switched almost overnight from hand-designed rules to learned features. Accuracy leapt.
  3. The transformer era (2017→). Attention let models use global context and scale to enormous data, taking over both language and vision.
  4. Foundation & promptable models (2021→). Instead of one model per task trained on a fixed label list, we got big models you prompt: CLIP (classify by text), SAM (segment anything you point at), Grounding DINO (detect anything you name). This is the single biggest shift the reports keep returning to: task-specific models → promptable foundation models.
  5. Vision-language models (2023→). Bolt a vision system onto a language model and you can simply ask an image questions in English (module 08).

Each module shows the modern approach next to what it replaced, so you see why the field moved.

Why licences appear in the tables

Open-source models come with legal terms. Some are permissive (Apache-2.0, MIT — free to use, including commercially). Some are copyleft (AGPL-3.0 — free to learn with, but commercial use requires either open-sourcing your own code or buying a licence). The reports record each model’s licence because for real projects it matters as much as the accuracy — and noticing it is part of becoming a practitioner.
